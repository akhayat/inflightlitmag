In the 45 years that Mrs. Jasmine Lim worked at the venerable Robertson's Department Store of Singapore (during which time she rose steadily from junior sales assistant (linen) to assistant senior cashier, receiving, along the way, nine Long Service Awards, 14 Letters of Commendation and six Employee of the Year nominations, four of the latter of which she lost narrowly to the late Madam Halimah, bless her soul), she also served four decades as a dutiful wife; three and a half of those decades were as a conscientious mother raising two sons and a daughter, while the last half-decade were spent as a doting (initially) grandmother of four grandchildren.

 

At the age of 65, Mrs. Lim finally retired two years after her husband had received his gold-plated watch. Liquidating a substantial portion of their combined pensions, the Lims looked forward to fulfilling their long-cherished dream of travelling the world. Truth be told, it was more Mrs. Lim's idea, having interacted as she did all her working life with retail items made in faraway lands (primarily China) and meeting thousands of customers, many of whom hailed from countries whose names she could not pronounce.

 

As fate would have it, six months into her retirement, when Mrs. Lim was painstakingly planning their grand expedition, Mr. Lim collapsed and died of a heart attack at the golf driving range. Upon hearing the news, Mrs. Lim's first reaction was one of mild annoyance, as she was in the midst of working out a complicated schedule of airport and rail transfers across Western Europe. Then, guilt took over and Mrs. Lim grieved over her loss accordingly.

 

Her grand plan to go abroad also seemed to be in some state of stasis. Her children could not tear themselves away from their families to accompany her for any amount of time. And though her retiree friends encouraged her to join them on short trips around Asia or even a weekend cruise to nowhere on a gambling ship, Mrs. Lim's zest to see the world beyond the shores of Singapore had greatly diminished, much to her dismay. Her globetrotting ambitions, it seemed, had also been consigned to ashes like that of the cremated remains of Mr. Lim.

 

After eight months as a widow, Mrs. Lim's three children took it upon themselves to revise her status. Gradually, before she realized it, the occasional weekday nights babysitting her grandchildren rose to five and a half days a week and, though she had the help of her own housekeeper, Mrs. Lim soon turned from doting (initially) grandmother to resentful nanny. She finally put her foot down and negotiated full weekends off twice a month from her grandmotherly/nanny duties.

 

With some time on her own, Mrs. Lim determined to explore, by herself, every nook and cranny of the Singapore city-state she lived in, district by district, street by street, even alleyway by alleyway. Every Sunday morning at precisely 8.00 am, armed with umbrella, cap, bottled water, chewy cereal bars, mobile phone, and public transport schedules she had carefully downloaded and printed, Mrs. Lim walked 10 minutes to the bus stop, the starting point of her weekly excursions.

 

Of late, Mrs. Lim had noticed an increasing number of foreign workers on the buses she boarded. They were mainly South Indian and Bangladeshi laborers working on the numerous building projects and construction sites in Singapore. Like Mrs. Lim, Sundays were also their day off, an eagerly awaited opportunity to shop for food and sundries, to meet up with their fellow compatriots, and, for the bolder among them, to engage in short-lived romantic dalliances with foreign female domestics.

 

The buses were usually full by the time they arrived at her stop, but there would always be someone polite enough to offer his seat to her, a courtesy Mrs. Lim gruffly accepted. That morning, her walk under the blazing tropical sun had made Mrs. Lim feel rather uncomfortable. After twenty unbearable minutes, the bus she had been waiting for finally trudged into the crowded bus stop with a weary sigh of its wheels.

 

Mrs. Lim boarded first with a speed that would have shamed many a century sprinter, tapped her transit card on the electronic card reader, and firmly pushed her way through the gauntlet of bodies to secure a seat at the back of the bus. She soon realized with dismay however that the air-conditioning unit in the bus was in dire need of servicing. She started to feel nauseous in the hot and stuffy surroundings, and her discomfort must have been obvious for she suddenly felt someone fanning her and an accented voice asking, "Are you all right, madam?"

 

"Yes, yes, I am fine, thank you," she answered after a few seconds, briefly embarrassed by her momentary weakness. She looked towards the voice, which belonged to a young Indian man of twenty who was standing next to her seat. He was neatly dressed in a crisp, pressed long-sleeved shirt. With the thick pair of glasses he had on, he looked rather a little nerdy.

 

Though she did not normally warm up even to relatives, let alone strangers, Mrs. Lim was soon chatting amiably with the young man whose name was Rajendran. While he had come to Singapore to work on a construction site a year ago, his fluency in English, thanks to a retired literature teacher who was a tenant in the house where he lived as a boy, had soon steered him to a supervisory post in the company he worked in.

 

Mrs. Lim decided to get off at the next stop and hail a taxi to take her home, and Rajendran offered to help her alight the bus. They stood in silence for a few minutes as they tried to flag a cab down.

 

Rajendran had assiduously continued to fan Mrs. Lim all this while, and the rolled-up travel map he was waving energetically in her face finally caught her eye.

 

"What map is that?" she asked a little hesitantly.

 

"It's a world map, madam," he replied with a grin. "I carry it everywhere with me. One day, I promise myself that I will save up enough money to go around the globe."

 

Mrs. Lim reflected in silence for a few moments; she said quietly, "That's interesting. You know, once upon a time, I wanted to ..." she paused, uncertain, recalling past memories, and the grand visions she once had of conquering distant lands.

 

"Perhaps we can chat about this; I have a few suggestions you might like," said Mrs. Lim, looking up at the young man who still had the stars in his eyes and the vigor of youth in his stride. She smiled at him and asked, "Now, let's sit down for some coffee and you can tell me where you would like to visit first."
