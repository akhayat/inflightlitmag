The pastor screamed at us
in a voice that shook
the holy rafters of the tiny church.
His harangue began
where fire and brimstone left off.
If ever a tongue could start a conflagration.
it was his.

"God is watching you!" he screamed.
"He's disgusted by your sinning!
You're all going to hell!"
The old folks in the congregation
would bend their heads,
their frail bodies overwhelmed with shame.
By the time God's bully was done,
there wasn't a Biblical figure
that we kids didn't fear.
My parents were clear on their responsibility.
A Sunday service was the verbal equivalent
of a thousand straps applied to a rear end.

Religion began like a tornado
but, when I was old enough,
I switched churches,
and it settled down into nothing more
than fine weather with occasional drizzle
and very rare squalls.
Preachers were typically more concerned
with fund-drives
than scarifying souls.
The sermons were wheat-toast.
The hymns even had a beat.

But the image of that
raving, ranting, screamer stayed with me.
For every bulletin of cake sales,
blood drives and shortfalls in the building fund
broadcast from the pulpit,
a demon-annihilator lurked behind.
I was still in his God's rifle sights
if I cursed under my breath,
or gave an attractive woman the once over.
My current, more accommodating almighty
gave me a free pass on anything short of murder.

Despite what the texts said,
I learned that Christianity is
far from monotheistic.
There's always been as many Gods as necessary.
There's the God who hates gays
and the one who loves them.
Also the God who recites the Pledge of Allegiance before breakfast
and his counterpart who waves "Great Satan" in America's face.
And what about the God whose first commandment clearly states,
'Thou shalt not take away a good man's AK47."

Many times I've heard the statement,
"If there were no God, we'd have to invent one."
If that was the case, we'd be barely getting started.
