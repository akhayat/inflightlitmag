What compelled her to drop to her stomach and drink from a puddle in the sand? They'd seen nearly every attraction the desert had to offer—Rock climbing, swimming in the oasis, and hikes in the dunes while the sun rose.
    Perhaps it was because drinking the water was the one thing you didn't dare try when you went places. You walked around with plastic bottles filled with designer water that everyone knows causes cancer (or something), and you came home to your conveniences feeling like you never got away from them.
    Whatever she hoped to gain, she must have found it, because as soon as that icy muddy water splashed down her throat, she was ready to go home.

    “2.15 a gallon for gas? Babe, get a picture of this. That's insane!” She fumbled with her phone's camera function for a second before handing it off to him to figure out. It wasn’t that she didn’t know how, just that she suddenly found the touch phone and its trendy interfaces tedious to look at, and she couldn’t summon the energy.
    He snapped the photo while she watched without interest.
    And she thought back to their first night in the desert, in their sleeping bags by the fire. At home, the night sky was dead and black, but people still pretended to marvel at it. In the desert, the sky was ablaze. Stars she’d thought were white burned orange or blue, and instead of sitting stationary, they ran and danced circles around one another. Beyond the campfire, the yipping of the coyotes turned to filthy jokes, while the owls screeched reprimands.
    She laughed at them, somewhere between sleep and wakefulness. Then she noticed the stranger who had come to get warm by their fire. It sat across from her, wrapped in a blanket and crowned with a broad cowboy hat. She couldn’t see its face when it turned to look at her, but its eyes shined black like a dog’s.
    “Come out with us,” it said in a scratchy voice, neither man nor woman. “Come have a drink.”
    But it was too cold to leave the sleeping bag.
    “Tomorrow,” she whispered, and she welled with excitement for the days ahead.

    But by morning the desert was lifeless, except for some dull-colored brush and sunning lizards. They sought out the “climbing rocks” their married friends had told them about. She snapped a photo of her fiancé striking a virile pose at the top.
    The lake was just where it was promised to be, and they both stripped down to their bathing suits and jumped in. From the water she saw what might have been a roadrunner. She was disappointed when a coyote on rocket-powered roller skates didn’t smash into the cliff wall.
    As they hiked back to camp, a hot wind started to blow her wet shorts-leg up her thigh.
    “Selfie!” her fiancé declared, and as soon as she saw what he was doing she held up her hand to try to shield her face. He snapped the shot. “This one’s going on Instagram,” he said.
    “I think it’s called an ‘usie’ if there’s two of us,” she said, laughing so she wouldn’t sound annoyed. “Man. No one told me the desert would be so hot,” she said, panting.
    “It should be okay,” he said. “As long as you keep hydrated.”

    In the afternoon they checked into a motel in town. The room was a dump, but it was air conditioned and within walking distance of Arnold’s Museum of Body Horrors. There was a glass case of extracted tumors the size of cantaloupes and one of mummified appendages. She took a photo of a skull with a long pig snout. According to the information card, the skull was human and once belonged to a man traveling under the stage name, “The Bald Beast.” The woman felt guilty for enjoying the exhibits so much.
    “We should go back to the desert tonight,” she said.
    But it rained hard that night, so they went back to the motel instead, soaking wet.

    Late that night there was a loud knock at the door. She looked.
    The door flung open.
    A darkened human frame stood in the threshold, only a little higher than the doorknob.
    She recognized the canine gleam in its eye.
    “What’s your name, dog-eye?” She asked.
    The figure stood there a long time but didn’t come inside.
    “How about that drink?” it said. “You promised.”
    “It’s a little late.”
    “I’m a brewer,” it said. “The first. The one that calls the water from underneath. It rises to the sand in pools, sweet with old clay.”
    The woman felt a sudden dread about this other presence, but somehow she knew she was safe from it inside the motel.
    “You came to my desert to search for something. My drink will give you power.”
    The stranger shut the door and was gone.

“2.15,” he said again, handing the phone back to her. “I was eleven the last time gas was that cheap.”
    She didn’t see what was so neat about it. Anyone could change the numbers on a stupid sign. She glared at the tin slabs, bared her teeth, and made them aware of what they were, silly markings signifying the whims of men in suits; men in suits who nobody liked because they were greedy and had meetings with the government in windowless rooms and wore suits. The five on the sign shrank from her and became a one. The two looked down and realized it was a zero, that it always had been.
    Her fiancé looked back up and dropped the gas hose. “What—”

    “I wanna play the slots,” she said.
    “Well. I guess we’re under budget for this trip, but don’t go crazy. We’re getting married, remember.”
    They had crossed into Nevada and were stretching their legs outside a casino.
    “Keep your money, you cheapskate,” she said. “I saw something over here.” Smelled it actually. Something in the great cement planters that smelled like sweat and sex and cocaine.
    “Got it!” And she snatched a five-dollar bill, rolled up and half buried in the planter soil.
    “Oh my god!” he said. “My baby’s got super powers.” He took her phone out of her purse. “Come on. We’re gonna document this miraculous sign. Right now.”
    She ignored him and he snapped the shot as she climbed the steps to the casino, waving her dollar bill in the air. The camera smeared her grin, turning her teeth into long fangs.
    “Hurry up!” she said. He lost her in the crowd that met him at the door. People were streaming to the roulette tables where someone was screaming excitedly. “Four in a row! Four in a row! Sweet baby Jesus, I love you!”
    “That’s an omen,” he heard her say, but still couldn’t find her behind the crowd.
    “Good or bad?” he asked.
    “Good for them, but that doesn’t leave a lot of luck in the room for us. Let’s do something before they waste it.”
    “Do what?”
    He saw her on the other side of the room, starting up the spiral staircase to the hotel rooms. He ran after her, sliding his hand on the shining banister.
    He reached the top in time to catch her disappearing down another hall. He jogged between numbered doors and forking corridors. Somewhere in the maze of halls, animals were snarling, the way dogs do when they mean to taste blood. He nearly jumped out of his skin when he realized that one of the snarling things was his fiancé.
    He found her glaring at one of the doors at the back of the hall. The door fell open, as if faltering under her gaze. He looked into the room that was obviously occupied, because the sheets were rumpled, and a cowboy hat lay at the foot of the bed.
    “I’m going to steal that hat,” she said.
    “No!”
    She went in. He squeezed his eyes shut. She was crazy. In a moment, a seven-foot tall mustachioed man with steer horns on his belt buckle was going to emerge from the bathroom and shoot them both.
    When he looked inside the room, she was nowhere to be seen. He whispered for her before looking over his shoulder and then creeping into the room.
    He looked around, hissed her name, but she didn’t respond. The bed and the occupant’s luggage obscured the back half of the room. She was either behind the suitcases or hiding under the bed.
    He climbed onto the bed. He leaned over the edge. Nothing. On the bright side, the bathroom door was wide open and no one was in it. Whoever was staying in this room obviously wasn’t around.
    Or so he thought, until a hand slid up his ankle and squeezed. Someone under the bed hummed in delight.
    “Don’t kick,” said the soft, scratchy voice. “Don’t scream.” He kicked. He screamed.
    He thrashed his leg, but the grip on his ankle tightened until his foot went numb. When he looked to the hallway for someone, anyone, his fiancé was standing in the threshold with the cowboy hat planted on her head.
    “Let him have your pants,” she said. He ripped off his zipper and kicked the pants free, then he and his fiancé tore down the hall.
    Wearing nothing under his pants had seemed like a good idea that morning. Someone told him it was like making love to the desert wind when it blew up your pant legs. But now he was on the stairs above a crowded casino, and he doubted very much that he could make it to the door with nothing hiding his genitals.
    That was when he noticed his fiancé cradling an open suitcase in her arms. It was stuffed with neat rows of dollar bills.
    “What did you take! That freak’s gonna chase us to the gates of hell to get that back!”
    “No he won’t,” she said. “It’s ours. As long as we don’t spend it.” She unbound a bundle of cash and tossed the bills over the banister. The gamblers watched the money float to the ground, but for a while, no one made a move or a sound, not even the machines. They stared at the money, they imagined climbing over backs and tearing off ears to grab their share.
    When the couple reached the front door the woman dumped the money on the velvet rugs, tossed the suitcase aside, and she and her fiancé hurried to their car before the casino could explode into a frenzy.
    Her fiancé pulled the hem of his shirt to hide his nakedness. The feel of that hand on his ankle seared in his memory. It only had three fingers, yet it held on so tight, so tight...
    “I left my keys in my pants,” he told her meekly.
    “I’ve got mine,” she said. “And by the way,” he turned to see her pointing a camera phone at him, “this one’s going on Instagram.”

He’d been driving all night when the sun came up over the Hollywood Freeway sign. Almost home.
    He looked at her, sleeping comfortably in the passenger seat, her face concealed by the brim of the stolen cowboy hat.
    “Hey, Natalie?” He said. “What was that all about?”
    Her eyes opened. They shined like a dog’s.
    On second thought, he didn’t want to know.
