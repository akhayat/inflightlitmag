He woke without waking and without the memory of ever falling asleep. He found himself in his bed, staring at the familiar spotty ceiling. The lights were dimmed, and everything looked disturbingly crooked, as if leaning over him. He was lying on his back with arms outstretched along his body, chin tilted up; every part of him felt heavy as lead.

He couldn’t move.

He started to panic, but it was distant--an out of body experience. His heart felt as if it were racing, and almost completely still. He was suffocating, but wasn’t--couldn’t struggle to breathe. It was as though he forgot how to do it properly; the air was still present around him, and nothing seemed to be blocking his nose or throat. He opened his mouth, welcoming the air with a slight gulp.

He was as confused as he’d ever been in his life, but instead of inhaling he exhaled something that was more than air. He kept doing that, transfixed. It wasn’t him working his lungs, pushing the air out, but something else in control of his body--an invisible force that drew the air from him. Stiff as a board and unable to breathe, he was more scared than a child after a vicious nightmare--scared like an adult who knows something is terribly wrong, and there’s no way to rationalise and fix it.

At some point, he felt a strange pressure on his torso, like someone was sitting on his chest. It startled him so much that his heart fluttered warningly; the next sensation would have paralysed him with a new wave of cold dread if he had not already been as still as a statue. He felt a touch to the inside of his palm, skin-on-skin contact, ice-cold fingers that he couldn’t warm up.

Time was non-existent in this state, but he still felt himself getting closer to the precipice of madness. Right when he was about to go over the edge, he started to feel the weight lift off of him, pulled from his very bones, escaping through his open mouth. The more it continued, the lighter and calmer he was. He fell; his own bed swallowed him up like a barrel full of tar. Wasting away felt awfully like a relief.

He came to his senses. He remained on his back as he stared into the waking sky which was grey and sombre with the remains of night. The air was cool and moist, smelled rotten-sweet, and despite it being thick with odours and slightly clogging his lungs, he was finally breathing. It felt wonderful. In his periphery, he could see tall grass and the outskirts of what appeared to be a forest. Once he became aware of his surroundings, the sounds rushed in: crickets and frogs and a weak splashing of water. He pushed himself up, leaned back on his elbows unsteadily, and saw a vast lake. The water was dark green and mostly still, impenetrable-looking. He couldn’t take his eyes away from it. He was completely enraptured and terrified by its mere presence.

He couldn’t possibly have missed something disturbing the surface--something big and dark and scaly, that caught the weak morning light with a gleam. It almost felt as if he were on his feet even before the thing disappeared under the water again. His eyes desperately scanned the lake, but it was tranquil again, keeping its secrets.

*

“I won’t keep it,” said the woman, no, the girl he never loved. And he knew by the conflict and, (disconcertingly enough) the challenge in her eyes, that she will as long as he were to stay with her.

Before that, before this point of no return, he met up with his best friend for the last time, just to say goodbye. He was being responsible. Other people already set up his life for him, the marriage, the moving, the job, a child. He felt so guilty, like such a failure. The plan was impossible to refuse. He just wanted to see his friend again so he could find the strength in her to keep his word. But now, after years since the event, he knew he’d secretly wanted to find the strength to stay with her. His friend was nineteen, taller and sturdier than him. Her hair was a complete mess. She had wide swimmer’s shoulders, angry eyes, freckles on her cheeks and the bridge of her straight nose. For him, she was the best part of every day. He never had the guts to kiss her.

This time he did, and she pushed him back, breaking his heart. But she went along with him a horrible second later, immediately rearranging the broken pieces by crowding him against a tree. Even with bark scratching his skin through a thin t-shirt, he could only concentrate on the touch of her hands on his forearms and the firm pressure of her chapped lips. Her hands were warm, with just a touch of coldness on the tips of her fingers, giving away that she too was nervous. The goosebumps crawled all over his body and left him giddy, nearly nauseous with excitement. The kiss was a bit overbearing--he couldn’t keep up. It was kind of like she was sucking the air straight from his lungs, killing him in the best way possible. He felt light-headed with it, delirious, stupid. His eyes were half-closed. Sunlight and undecipherable colours were streaming under his eyelids, overly bright and cheerful. His heart was ready to burst from happiness.

It was a dream, but he could have had it, if only he were braver then. In reality, he left her with an awkward one-armed embrace; her face was schooled into a mask of indifference. But her eyes were still stormy, like always. She was never calm and never uncaring, or maybe she only was like that with him and he simply didn’t know any different. He knew she was disappointed and hurting and possibly all the things he felt in that moment too, but just like him she ended up resigning. He wished she’d made the first move, crumbling his resolve, choosing for him.

But this time around he made it right. He was being responsible.


“Okay,” he said to the one he didn’t love, like he was supposed to, because he was seventeen and scared and not ready and so in love with someone else. She gave him a long and hard stare, betrayed, and he knew he failed someone who was relying on him. But this way he would have no child to fail in the future.

He found himself sitting a safe distance from the same lake. A fishing rod was fixed on the ground near his hip, the buoy idly floated on the surface of the water.. He felt tired and sleepy, as if he were grudgingly waking from a ten minute sleep. His body was heavy and aching. He must have been sitting in this position for a long time. He didn’t know how he ended up fishing, but his sluggish mind began to buzz just from the thought of what he intended to catch.

The unknown figure that briefly showed over the surface of the water taunted him and hid again underwater-- a place he couldn’t possibly go.

Maybe it got away, he thought to himself.

To him, the lake appeared as a deep abyss. It was everlasting and empty, yet filled with dangers--a breathless and soundless place, as frightening and as hypnotising as the night sky and open space it leads to. The more he stared at it, the more it filled him with its enigmatic voice. His skin crawled with indecipherable whispers, his blood screamed relentlessly, as if the lake was speaking through him while it remained deceptively quiet and aloof like a tomb.

His thoughts were interrupted by a rupture in the water; the buoy bobbed invitingly. And then, after a pause, it violently submerged. The fishing line extended and disappeared, quickly swallowed by the water. No longer controlled by his shocked paralysis, he snatched the fishing rod, gripped the reel handle, and rolled what was left of the line. The creature on the hook was strong and tenacious, but seemingly playful and vivacious in a childish way. It released its hold, let him roll the wheel for several seconds, and (with growing confidence) lull him into a false sense of security. It started to tug again, unexpectedly and strongly. With every pull it nearly ripped the spinning rod from his hands.

And then it did, the handle snapped. The rod practically flew out, spinning in the air. It tumbled into the water with a sharp splash. And even before the meaning of it filled him with disappointment, the water raptured again. The thing he was trying to catch showed itself, and it wasn’t nearly as big as he expected. It was human-like, small and underdeveloped--skeletal and malnourished. It was a child, with grey slimy skin partially covered by flakes, some of which were falling off as if the creature were sick and losing them. The child from the lake barely had hair, but several dark rusty-red strings among the scales on its head and face. Its big green orbs aggressively glowed through the cracks.

He took a step towards the water, raised his arm, and reached for the creature. It moved again, quickly and gracefully--like a sleek green ribbon on the wind. It was surprisingly ferocious and powerful for someone so fragile and sickly-looking. It flipped itself, showed a fish tale, shabby but still glistening on the light like emeralds. It went back underwater, something final about the loud splash and the following silence. The child gripped his heart with a cold glove, with the promise of no return.

*

This particular day was always on his mind, but until now he could never imagine it as clear and real as it was then. Somehow, reliving it felt like a gift instead of torture. He sat beside her hospital bed and watched her, his eyes greedily tracing the features he started to forget.

She slept, serene. Her shaggy ginger hair scattered on the pillow; her freckles were stark on her sickly white skin--her body unexpectedly fragile in a baggy medical gown in this unresponsive state. Her breathing was raspy and clattering, but faint. He placed his hand inside her open palm, and promised that everything will be okay. He thought she’d have been truly fine, if he had stayed with her that day, years ago. They both would have had such different lives. It’s a pointless regret, but he couldn’t help feeling it--the claws scratched at his heart. Not hard enough to penetrate it, but just enough to make it bleed. He followed his moral compass instead of his gut instinct. He took responsibility. But sometimes, not running away is running away. He should have stayed behind, where his heart was.

He lightly squeezed the hand he was holding, and she squeezed his back upon waking. For several moments they just stared at each other, like she was, too, relearning his face. His heart, the traitor, connected all the wires between what she once was--how he stored her inside his head when he walked away--and what she is now, in front of him. He fell in love all over again, quickly and irresponsibly. She never took any of his shit, and he was thankful for that. “People who love you should accept you the way you are”--it sounds nice, but certainly doesn’t work with assholes. She straightened him out, made him less flawed. He smiled, while he remembered.

It wasn’t like that when it actually happened. He sat beside her bed, she didn’t move; a white sheet was sprawled over her body. He pulled it off and revealed her face. He needed to see another person he failed. The vacant look in her cloudy eyes made him shiver. Her head was mangled after the car accident. No deformity and no time could possibly make him feel any different about her. It isn’t normal, he thought. Love was a chronic illness that spoiled his life. It never went away--always on his mind. I was part of everything he did. When he touched her hand, it was neither entirely cold nor wooden yet, but it was slack and cooling, lifeless. It did not allow him to pretend. He gripped it nonetheless, lifted it slightly from the bed. It was just a dead weight in his hold.

He fell on his knees on the balding edge of the shore. The grass was scarcer and thinner there, the ground under it soft and squishy. It immediately left wet patches on his thin pants and passed cold through his bones. Heart in his throat, he lowered his shaking hands in the dark water. They looked pearly-white and shapeless there, unnaturally glowing. After witnessing something inhuman, monstrous some would say, that was the first time he saw something truly evil. Not just the light hiding underwater, but also the sight of his own completely useless hands.

Suddenly, he was in his bed again, and felt the air forced back inside of him.He was reminded of the times when an open mouth in the wind caught him off-guard, momentarily choked, bunched up in his lungs. This time it was all wrong too, no resolution. It filled him like a blow-up doll. He expanded with it but did not come to life. His lungs were heavy and sagged, all clumped up, resting on the bottom of his overflowing ribcage. He was dead.

He opened his eyes to his lips moving, forming words, addressing his family. His daughter waved him off and laughed. “Stay close to the shore,” he thought he said, because she was little and didn’t know how to swim, because he’ll always be worried about her, regardless of how old or strong and capable she got. He was always a hard worker, but work consumed him as an adult, which led to him and his family barely seeing each other. But in this memory, it was almost startling to be so close to his daughter--to see her so lively, having a good time, smiling openly and radiantly, as happy as she could be in a household where the parents don’t love each other. Her dark hair was wet and dripped from all the splashing. There was an unfamiliar spark in her consistently sad eyes.

He always thought he turned away for just a minute, engaged in the argument with his wife. The challenge in her resentful eyes, the only times when she’s the centre of his undivided attention; but it was a minute too long. When he looked back to the lake, his daughter was nowhere to be seen. She was underwater, he realised with a shocked delay, his heart stopping momentary. He froze and stumbled, like a toddler making his first steps.

He breached the surface, dunked inside the waterbody, its guts dark and obscure. It took him unforgivably long to find his drowning daughter. She was unconscious, and when he wrapped his arms around her and pulled her up, she was scarily heavy. When he resurfaced with her still body in his arms, she almost slipped. His wife was screaming. There was a  ringing noise in his head.

He put his daughter’s small body on the ground and began performing CPR, his hands and breathing shaking, desperate. He forced air in her watery lungs while still struggling to properly breathe himself. He pushed on her narrow brittle chest. It was covered in a sparkly green one-piece swimsuit. In that moment, he wasn’t sure if he was doing enough or too much. He was simultaneously scared of both his strength and his carefulness. It wasn’t long till she flinched and started violently coughing out the water, but to him it felt like it aged him. He laughed himself stupid and listened to her life with hysterical tears in his eyes.

In the past, where it was real and his actions mattered, he tried very hard, but couldn’t bring her back. He couldn’t deny she was gone, and the hopelessness suffocated him. He thought she was taken away because he didn’t deserve it, even a sliver of happiness. He wanted to hit himself then, because while it felt like her death was his fault, this wasn’t about him.

*

The next time he came to himself, the water was up to his knee. The shock of it was so unexpected and scary. it froze him in place. An irrational childish part of him told him that he’ll be okay as long as he didn’t move. The water was still enough around him, it wouldn’t notice his presence. It still felt like a silent threat around him, like he stood on a raising chest of a sleeping monster.

When he calmed himself enough, the first thing he noticed was the lonely buoy in the middle of the lake. It jumped slightly, almost like it shivered in fright, expecting him to pounce and catch it. He felt a strange exciting surge of a gamble, a sick curiosity pushing his fear down. He moved towards the buoy. Once he was close enough to touch it, he noticed for the first time that there was no string extending from it. Holding his breath, he reached and took the seemingly harmless piece of plastic between two of his fingers. He tugged it up and finally saw the string. To his surprise, it went down and didn’t just float freely in the water. It strained. He pushed his head underwater, but saw no bottom. There was only the bleak whiteness of his constantly moving legs with all shades of green dying out in heavy and clotting black molasses.

He resurfaced and tugged on the fishing line. He struggled with it and cut his hands. Blood dropped in the water and disappeared instantly, but the stinging pain only made him hold on harder and pull faster. It felt a bit like what he imagined getting the moon would be like after you lassoed it. The thoughts inside his head were an agitated jumbled mess. He thought that maybe he’s about to be caught on a metal hook and dragged underwater to be served on someone’s table like a fish. Or maybe any second now he’d pull the plug out and the entire world would be sucked into a pit at the bottom of the lake. He laughed hysterically at that last thought, he couldn’t stop himself. When the truth revealed itself, it was in some way even more shell-shocking and strange. He fell completely silent, unsure.

His hands trembled on a newborn child he just pulled from the water. It was drenched, naked, cool to the touch, and ghostly pale with spots of grey hardened skin. The sight seized his heart in immediate grip of instinctive bone-deep terror and grief he couldn’t possibly explain. He was shaking all over now, bile in his throat, his heart raced and occasionally skipped a beat, confused and uncertain and so deeply, irreversibly hurt.

What was he supposed to do? It felt like nothing existed apart from this cold lake. Time had stopped on this foggy morose morning and the sun would never fully come up again. He had nowhere to go, no one to share his pain with. An unnamed inescapable force urged him to put the baby back in the water, like it belonged there. But he refused to do it; he held it to his chest, breathed in the decaying smell of lake water, savoured it. He could practically feel the last semblance of strength leaving his body. It seeped out from him like a mixture of blood and water.

He was almost ready to go down himself--to just fall back and let the lake envelop them both. Anything to not leave this child alone, to not be left alone himself. Just before he let himself go, he felt the movement, a weak touch to his chest. He froze in bewilderment and tentative hope. The baby unmistakably squirmed in his arms and tugged at the collar of his wet shirt. He pulled it back from himself and stared down at it, the child staring back, eyes big and blue and sad. It was alive, she was alive, and his heart hurt even worse, his chest so tight, he himself wanted it to burst open. He couldn’t help it; he closed his eyes and sobbed, hard and unrelenting, like there was nothing that could possibly console him, like he could never stop now.

Somehow this child was his daughter, he knew it, like he knew now that the sickly looking scaly thing was his best friend. Remembering them, people his heart ached for, ones he’d lost, almost felt like a physical transformation. He finally became himself, filled to the brim with losses. He hunched down from their added weight. He couldn’t possibly be a man still capable of hope and levity, a man that didn't miss his daughter every second of his life. He might as well have drowned her with his inadequateness, his carelessness. He dreamed of doing it, of putting her in water, almost every night since he put her in the ground.

He opened his eyes to a swarming jet-black mass. It felt alive around him with its breathing and relentlessly moving, It almost seemed like it got closer, expanded, as it reached to him. He leaned into it and touched it, dived in. He felt himself disintegrate at the edges as he became one with it.

Floating in the darkness made him think.

His world went dull once, lost all intensity, and he drifted through the motions; he lived every day clumsily without putting his heart in it, and eventually he honestly wished life was a dream world--inconsequential--a set of nameless rooms and places all morphing into each other. Just like in a dream you, could leave them anytime you wanted, and no one would notice. No one would stop or call you back. Once you closed the door, what was left behind it wouldn’t matter, wouldn’t even exist anymore. He wanted his life to be like that, with a leeway, an easy way out--with the ability to just wake when you’ve had too much.

He was weak-hearted or felt too strongly, either way he earned to be untethered and independent from things he knew will break him--to be like a ship in the open sea having nothing except shimmering water on the horizon. He knew a life like that would have been lonely, but he knew loneliness already, shades of it: the pressure, the miles of going by himself before falling into step with someone else and still having nothing to say. There was no getting used to it, but he knew what to expect--something similarly empty, but more blank than hollow--a solitude from beginning to the end. A one-man journey. He learned the hard way that people you care about are not the ones who sail with you but the riffs that sooner or later wreck your ship.

Somehow being all alone where he was now, in his dark apartment full of memories and things that don’t belong to him, knowing no one’s footsteps will join him now, was worse than risking everything and waiting for an impending wreckage. But it was selfish of him, to choose the pain. While they were his losses, they were other people's’ lives. His death would only help the world further forget the ones who passed from it.

He resurfaced from the blackness, slowly and heavily, wrapped around the large scaly thing from the lake. It was strangely warm, pulsing in his arms, especially to his own cold and stiff body. The blackness stopped moving and hardened, a bed inside a room, and he was straddling the thing’s chest--stopping it from taking deep breaths. His hands crawled to its neck of their own accord and gripped it. He felt the fallen flakes stick to his palms and prick his skin. He wrapped his fingers around the thing’s neck, and it opened its mouth, there was just an endless tunnel of darkness inside. It was the first time he could see its face clearly. It was hardly human now, after the transformation, but still recognisable. His hold went slack but he did not let go, startled by the revelation but not discouraged.

And wasn’t it particularly cruel that the only thing from all the things he was made to relive and could possibly change was his own death? That was the only thing he was fine with. For a second his fingers constricted, pressed even harder into the vulnerable flesh of his own throat. More scales fell, the skin under them sleek and grey. His own eyes, unexpectedly human on a disfigured, sharpened face, stared back at him. They begged, starkly blue--the same shade as his daughter’s--and he wanted to kill this pathetic worthless thing so much; he was vibrating, salivating with it.

He stopped, disgusted with himself--with his weakness--not with his ruthlessness. In no way was his holding back mercy. He used to always choose the easiest way--way out, way in, roads already paved for him, people ready to hold his hand through thunderstorms. He was reckless by living carefully, not taking sharp risks, wallowing in his misery to the point of always feeling numb and disconnected, He was so deeply unhappy in his own skin an eventual tragedy felt almost inevitable. Wrong choices lead to bad consequences, and like always he was unable to deal with them. He didn’t deserve the oblivion of death. He let himself go. His fingers slid over angry bruises, and fell back on the bed. And as his dream self took his first deep but shaky and wet breath, liquid cluttering inside his monstrous chest, somewhere else he cracked his eyes open, letting sunlight and indecipherable colours in.

The people you care about, love, are not riffs, he realised, they’re sirens, and instead of you coming upon them by chance, they surf the waters looking for you. They don’t tear your ship to shreds, you leave it willingly, jump overboard to be with them, to be one of them. The charming voices you follow--all the love you’re capable of--end up being your undoing, and just like that you’re a victim, not an equal. But to them, to them you’re their siren.

He wakes, and the first thing he registers is his back against the ground, the right side of his face in a puddle of something sticky and wet and unpleasantly cold. The smell comes with a delay, but when it does come, it practically devours his nostrils and fills his throat with bile. He scrambles on his knees, hovers above the ground, and leans on it with his hands. His open palms kiss the spit-smothered pavement while he retches, chocking and trembling. Even more blood, thick and dark, emerges from his mouth and burns his swollen tongue, dry and heavy like a piece of cotton.

That day he buried his daughter in a horribly small coffin, under five feet of cold ground. He’d been heavily drinking till the sun came down, and then some more. The night had found him in the dark alley, puking his guts out, and the dreamless sleep he’d had after was the last good one, the calm before the storm.

He wakes again, and it’s to his wife’s face, pale, her eyes stricken. He’s been avoiding her like the plague since the funeral. It only intensified his guilt, like even under cover of his plausible deniability he still blames himself for them both. Last week she packed a small suitcase and went to live with her parents. Said she can’t physically stay in the house and sounded repelled with herself. He let her go with no war in his heart, and that alone finally cemented what he always knew--he hadn’t learned to love her.

Right now they’re on the floor of his--their--bedroom. She’s on her knees and crouching, desperately searching his face. He’s lying in the puddle of his own fresh vomit. He thinks she dragged him from the bed, so encompassed by fear she wanted to get him to the bathroom first. The faulty logic of doing that caught up with her and she just stuck her fingers down his throat, making him puke out his poor breakfast, stomach acid and all the pills he took. He lifts his head slightly, looking at her, finally looking right at her face, and there’s no disappointment and no judgement and no hate in her big frightened eyes. He coughs, still unused to breathing, and she looks at him all wrong, like she’s seeing a miracle.

This time, he stays.
