You’re sitting there at the bus stop, and you’ve been waiting for over an hour, right?  The damn buses in L.A. are never running on time. You take out your phone to check the time, and a bright green 10 stares back at you. But it’s not the right number, see. Check it again. You’d swear on your life it said the same thing yesterday and the day before that.
   
You’re kind of fed up with the whole thing actually, and you toss the stupid thing into the street, feeding it to the cars. Maybe you’ll get a bite and catch one with a pretty girl in it. But the Voice in Your Head shows up again, standing next to you.  He looks like Mephistopheles, chiding you while still functioning like some kind of self-aggrandizing extension of your awareness of the world. “You shouldn’t have done that,” he advises. “How in the fuck are you gonna keep good time now?”

“I guess I’ll just have to keep a running count from now on,” you reply, staring straight ahead, the way you always do.

You get to about a hundred and six from that moment when the bus finally arrives.  You and the Voice in Your Head – who now looks very much like Tanto reluctantly but dutifully following you – slither on with your heads down, feeling the lingering friction of a previous confrontation that had left a sore draft in the air.


II

When you wake up, you’re astonished to find that the running count in your head stands at a bemusing six hundred and thirty. You didn’t expect to add more than a couple hundred, so you’re not surprised to see that you’ve missed your stop in as many miles. The Voice in Your Head is staring vehemently out the window, looking like Joan of Arc in her impatience.

You both get off at the next stop and notice that the air breathes differently in this part of town. You have to suck it in and blow it out a few times to get used to the sourness of it, and you suspect it’s a deliberate attempt to throw intruders like you off guard. The Voice in Your Head, looking like Marie Curie with her empiricism, confirms this.
“I used to know someone around here,” she says, lightly sniffing the air while catching something familiar in the scent. “She can make a real good hamburger, I can tell you that much.”



III

The running count in your head is somewhere in the one thousand two hundreds by the time the two of you are climbing a beaten staircase, flanked on both sides by peeling walls and a stench like sewage that permeates the nostrils with a sledge hammer, a savage, distant cousin of the weird draft you got outside.

You’re greeted at the door by a man with a severed head, or rather, a headless body, because you don’t know where the head is right away. Your acquaintance (I think her name was Susan or the like) is stretched out on a table in the corner. Her teeth are clenched tight and yellow, beaming their unseemliness in all directions as she thrashes this way and that. Her limbs are out-stretched and lightly seizing, and her blood sugar’s low.

The Voice in Your Head, head cocked lightly to one side, looking like Alexander the Great in his ambition, leans coyly over the woman, cooing, “Perhaps I can wake this beauty with a kiss?”

“Don’t waste your time,” demands a voice from the other side of the room. It is the head of your door greeter, perched atop a vast dining room table, grinning contemptuously while the body tenderly feeds it Caesar salad.  “She doesn’t think she needs a prince or knight in shining dregs. Watch her writhe and twist on the table there. She thinks she’s the Junkie Jesus, kicking violently for all of mankind. Why, just look at her arms! They’re like a wretched Pollock, pock marked red lines crisscrossing all up and down like that. Just listen to me, and I can tell you that she’s more Achilles than Christ.”

You start to nod your head and then stop yourself, thinking it somehow rude. The Voice in Your Head looks down, depressed and defeated. He is Hunter S. Thompson right before the end, holding on to nothing more than nostalgia for a time that will never come again.


IV

You exchange grim goodbyes and walk back into the street. Your running count is almost two thousand and a half when you realize that you're still hungry. You and the Voice in Your Head pick out a rundown place with a Vietnamese family that sells hamburgers with a Coke and side of fries for six dollars.  

You gaze out the window to see a row of long, black funeral cars lazily sliding down the road at a reserved crawl, and you swear you can hear the dead inside pounding against their casket lids, chanting in unison that old American anthem: “Fuck, fuck, fuck!”
