The wallpaper was beautiful: pale yellow, with smiling teddy bears holding balloons at regular intervals. As soon as I picked it up, I knew it’d be perfect for the baby room. The yellow was soft, like early morning sunshine, and the teddy bears looked soft and inviting too. Their little cartoon noses, snub and round, looked out underneath soft, shining brown eyes. The balloons were shiny too. There was a green one, a blue one, and a pink one.
“It doesn’t even matter if it’s a girl or a boy,” I murmured to myself.
A passing saleslady stopped.
“Do you have a question?” she asked, looking over the rims of her thickly-framed reading glasses. “Can I help you with something?” She was older and impatient-looking, with angry gray roots pushing out from her ugly head. The hair dye was fading fast, making the red highlights garish and all wrong for a woman pushing sixty.
I shook my head and turned away from her. I looked at the wallpaper sample on the wall again. It made me smile. It was a relief to turn away from the glowering eyes of the saleslady to the quiet contentment of those bears. I could almost hear the lullaby playing from the music box. I could just see our beautiful, bouncing baby lying in the crib, little hands reaching out towards the funny little bears with their jaunty balloons.
I picked up the rolls of wallpaper and set them down in the basket, then made my way to the cash register.
It was Big Red. I smirked to myself. “Big Red.” That was funny.
Red flashed me a tight smile.
“Find everything ok?” she said, her tone cold and condescending.
“Just fine,” I said, challenging her with my gaze.
She took her time ringing it up. After the second roll, her eyes squinted down and she looked closely at the label on the wallpaper, putting it up to her reading glasses.
“Miss, you do know this is a close out?” she said, looking at me with her impertinent cow’s eyes.
“What?” I said. What did that even mean, a “close out”?
“I mean, there’s nothing left after this. It’s discontinued,” she said, her tone metallic. She was a total robot, I decided. I had to stop myself from taking a step back. She was hard and made of metal, a complete automaton. I wanted nothing to do with her.
“Do you have enough wallpaper for the room you want to use it in?” she inquired, her voice grating on my ears.
“Stop it!” I said, the words ripping out of me before I could stop them. Big Red’s eyebrows shot up. “I mean,” I continued, suppressing the shake in my voice, “it’s fine. It’s not a big room.”
The woman smiled. Of all things! She smiled!
“A nursery?” she said, nodding. “That’s fine.”
“None of your business,” I said. I couldn’t help it! That grin. That self-satisfied grin made me want to hurt her.
She gave me a sidelong glance and finished ringing up the wallpaper. Finally.
I gathered up the bags with the rolls of wallpaper and walked home. The shop wasn’t far from the apartment, and it was good for me—for us—to walk. Good for the baby, I thought to myself—and I smiled.
Good for the baby.
I wanted to shout it from the rooftops!
I could feel myself glowing as I walked up the stairs to the third floor, carrying the bags of wallpaper. I had to pass crotchety old Mr. Hankinson with his broccoli breath on the second floor landing.
“Good day, Miss Gill,” he said. He flashed his old geezer smile, but I wasn’t fooled. He was a pervert. I saw him looking up my skirts every time I ascended the stairs. But today I was wearing pants—fooled him! I pretended to say hello and hoofed it up the last set of stairs.
It was with relief I entered the apartment. I was the first one home, as usual.
I set down the bags on a chair and put my purse on the kitchen counter. I had forgotten to get the mail, but I would worry about that later. I wanted to pin some of the wallpaper to the wall of the baby room to see how it would look—I had to see it!
I riffled through the bags and pulled out a roll of it. In the baby room—well, it was really a walk in closet. But it would do, at least for a while. I turned on the light and ran a nail across the tight, clear plastic holding the wallpaper roll together. The plastic covering floated to the floor, like a thin snakeskin. With a sense of majesty, I flapped the roll of wallpaper outward, unravelling the roll of winking teddy bears.
They were lovely.
I took a push pin and pinned the sheet of wallpaper to the wall, pushing aside the winter coats and summer dresses.
There they all were; rows of neat little teddy bears with their funny little balloons pulling them ever upward. Evenly spaced on a background of yellow like a lemon. Or was it like the summer sun? Or like an egg yolk…
I narrowed my eyes. In the light of the closet, the wallpaper looked…off. It looked less sun-shiny and more…beige. Was there dirt on this particular roll of wallpaper? Maybe it was defective? The woman at the store had said it was discontinued—but why would she have sold me a defective roll?
I knew there had been something off about the saleslady. She was probably doing this to spite me. Throw in some nasty rolls of wallpaper, tainted wallpaper. On second thought, the bears looked wrong too. Their eyes were not so happy—they looked angry. Like little black buttons, heartless and soulless. The strings they held from the balloons were like little nooses.
I could feel a slight dizziness come over me; the ground seemed to tilt just slightly and my throat tightened. Quickly I left the closet and, with quickening steps, I ran to the other rolls of wallpaper. I split open the plastic cellophane on each of them one by one, and each one burst from its packaging like a toy snake from a joke can of peanuts. Just like the joke snake, there was nothing funny about the unfurling rolls of sickening, yellow wallpaper.
They were all tainted. None of them were the same as what I had seen at the store. They were a dark, smoggy, ugly yellow, the yellow of sulphur and rotten egg yolks. The bears were grinning evilly at me, and their sharp, dark eyes were dead and black. I groaned. This was all awful. It was worse than awful—it was horrifying.
The bears had to die. I started to rip them up, first slowly, then more and more quickly. All the bears. They all had to die. I had to rip them up. They were awful. They were staring at me with their dark beady eyes. The wallpaper cut my hands so I bled from tiny, smarting cuts, but I didn’t care—I had to kill them all so their eyes wouldn’t follow me.
Suddenly, there was the sound of the key in the lock and the door was opening.
“Charlotte?”
It was John—my very own John, home from work.
He looked at me, and I didn’t have time to hide the wallpaper. He saw it and his eyes widened.
“Please,” I said. “Don’t say anything!”
He could only look at me and stare.
“Don’t look at me either!” I said.
“What is all this stuff?” he said, finally, looking down at the floor, littered with the scraps and remains of the hideous bears and the stifling yellow wallpaper. He picked up a larger piece of it, rubbing the thick paper in between his thumb and forefinger.
I couldn’t move. I watched him, watched him looking at the wallpaper, staring at it, thinking about it.
“I’m sorry!” I said finally, the words rushing out of my mouth like the rolls of wallpaper rushing out of their plastic wrappings. “I saw the bears at the store, and I wanted them for the nursery but then I got them home and they were terrible. Terrible bears.”
His eyes widened even more, and he stepped towards me.
I took a step back.
“Please don’t be mad,” I said. “I just wanted it for the nursery.”
“You know—” he started.
I cut him off.
“Don’t say it,” I said, my voice a whisper, barely a sound in my throat. I looked away from him, down at my hands, my bleeding hands that seemed, now, slightly yellow from the dye on the wallpaper.
“You lost the baby,” he said, quietly, his voice full of sadness.
“I know,” I whispered. I couldn’t look at him. I just kept looking at the beautiful wallpaper lying in ripped drifts on the floor.
“I just couldn’t help it,” I whispered. “The wallpaper was so beautiful in the store.”
