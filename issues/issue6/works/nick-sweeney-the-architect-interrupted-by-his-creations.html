A wall-eyed boy stands in my doorway. I don’t know how long he has been there. I think how without the scale dictated by the doorway, he would look like no boy at all; could be a colossus, or an insect.
            “What do you want?” The timbre of my voice surprises me. Sometimes I must resist a mania to repeat utterances, just to make sure the voice is my own. I ask again, and the boy steps in.
            I have a recollection of him lifting his head from his food, looking like a metal automaton depicting a lizard. I was the only one at the table who saw this. I started out of my chair, and they all looked at me when I pointed, saying, “Can he be switched off?”
            There is a racing car spread out over my reception room. Its frame and parts are like those of some magnificent beast reduced to dissection.  What kind of boy would look so pointedly at me, when he could be marvelling over an Amilcar from the future past – it is the very same Amilcar sports model whose wheel broke the neck of dancer Isadora Duncan when she caught her foolish scarf in its delicate spokes. I could point out to the boy that it was only then that she achieved the pure classical form she had sought all her life, and sometimes in the chrome I see her image as still and perfect as the Winged Nike from the island of Samothrace, and as broken.
            The boy raises a thumb to cue a wedding band wheezing into the afternoon. Outside on the square, tenement crones raise eyes, trying to see the music. Why bother, I want to call. Heralds in yellow velvet and white lace turn into the square, themselves heralded by a fog of mosquitoes, some of whom will find their way under bridal veils, waistcoats and eyelids, and into photos. The camera will freeze them in their bloodlust, taking their place in all time, here in Trievo.
            The tourists used to ooh and aah about the light in Trievo. They didn’t realise, though, that light is no good unless it falls on brick, stone, glass, metal; it is only these that give the light any property. You can’t look at beauty without this backdrop of hard materials, products of toil and fire, and the will that forced them into being. People don’t get married in churches because they are in awe of God; they marry there for the photos, and a share of the light. 
            By afternoon, that light rests on the starkest features of what during the war was known as the Avenue of Painted Women; these are the bricked-in windows of the casa grande, originally belonging to the Medici, though I suspect that its sole inhabitant was Gandolfo Medici, referred to usually as ‘the Unfortunate’. After displaying his madness in his teens, he was sent to Trievo in the hope that death would claim him before he could claim his inheritance. He frittered away a lot of it all the way to the age of thirty, though, when the mosquitoes annoyed him into stepping off the roof into a different oblivion. 
            The casa grande found its way into our family. Some say that Gandolfo Medici’s seed also found its way there, but I’m not so sure of that; our mild madness is one-toned and even, lacks the Medici stamp of savagery.
            Commentators talk about the mosquitoes, and the stagnant air in summer, the threat of plague that keeps the tourists away in, presumably, their millions. They talk about acquaintances foolish enough to croak their last summers away here, talk with awe of whatever detained them in the graveyard outside the Byzantine walls, insect bites, rat bites, or just the pale yellow mist immediately before sunset, which comes down the streets like mustard gas, chased merrily by black clouds of biting insects. The chattering classes document Trievo in the travel supplements, and yatter about its beauty, yet dare one another to pass a week here.
            It was the plague that killed my Roman ancestors. One of the satirists wrote a verse about the town abandoned to the sickness of sleep, and nobody alive to collect the dead. He traced the fire that ruined all that lay inside the walls to the last man alive, a baker busy making funeral cakes up to the moment when he heard a buzzing in his ears and felt his vision dim, and called to his dead wife that he was quite unwell, quite, quite unwell, and that he was going to lie down and take a – .
            That’s nature given free rein for you. But it’s nothing without architecture. Without towers, there is no point in looking at the sky. Without walls, and the apertures cut into them, there is really no point to light; without roofs, no majesty or cruelty in winter, no places in which to wrap up and linger in bed; no joy in frost if it doesn’t cover the protective skin of glass.
            Without the mosquito clouds of summer, my father Don Argento would never have made his fortune from his research into that noble little bloodsucker, his publications that keep those tourists out to the ruin of would-be restauranteurs and peddlers of souvenirs; even the pickpockets have to take working holidays to graduate. With no mosquitoes, Don Argento would never have passed that fortune on to me when he got bitten by one too many of the opportunistic little beasts, and died yellow and shaking.
            When I came back from my travels, Don Argento never wanted to know what I had seen, nor about who I had met. “The world is a bad and corrupt place,” he would state. “And you have become a bad and corrupt man. You will stay here,” he commanded, when the time came for me to seek out the world again. He sent his servants after me. At the station, they beat me and kicked me till my teeth shook, but they could never hold me. 
            Don Argento left me his money, but he shrank my world; one condition, enshrined in a codicil policed by curtain-twitching lawyers, was that I give up on that world, and keep to my home in Trievo.
            In a time before that, I made towers rise out of southern hemisphere hellhole cities, commissioned by dictators with money to burn. They are now filled with the remnants of banks and businesses whose financial whizzkids fled or were butchered. I raised follies in an absurd Italianate town in the east of Europe, and made a monument to the dead of world wars. Their voices sang to me over the sound of diggers, in gratitude, I thought in my vanity, but they were really screaming out the frustration of those who fought to have a fair world built around them, instead of some chunk of masonry.
            I built an opera house in a minor northern city. I met the Duce, was held steady in the blank-eyed gaze of a fat child. I fashioned the shell for him, went back for the first night, saw the place ruined with plush and perfume. A diva had to restrain me from going up to the nation’s brother and yelling, “I brought you the future, and what have you done? You’ve turned it into the past.” But that was the paradox of Mussolini: he was a nineteenth century man, shoving our country into the twentieth century at the point of a revolver. As a young man, I didn’t respect paradox – it was an eccentric, Greek way of looking at the world. I was out in that world as a Roman. The diva gripped my shoulders as I watched the leader declare the place open by leaning from his seat and barking at the musicians, “Now, play!” I was saddened to see him at the opera, in his dress suit and spiv spats, his bowler hat on his podgy knees.
            I liked the diva’s hands on my shoulders. I screwed her in a hallway to the twee sound of Mozart, made her sing just for me. I told her how the only way Mozart could be improved would be to add a hundred roaring motorbikes to his toodlings. I felt a current between us. In this way I forgot about the Duce and how he had worked the impossible with the force of his petulant, child’s will, worked his wishes on the world. And on my opera house. The shadow of my jealousy came back to me next morning like a hangover, and I remembered the diva telling me that the Duce had screwed her first, and had screwed her quicker – not a man who liked to hang around – and better.
            Mesmerised by the gleam of my father’s fortune, I forgot the world. It was easier to forget than Don Argento’s servants. One of them followed Gandolfo Medici off the roof while searching for a nightingale whose song had been keeping me awake. The other had an incident with a fruit cake that contained ground glass; he didn’t have to eat the whole thing. Actually, he did. I was holding a pistol to his head at the time.
            With revenge out of the way, I was able to concentrate on the finer things. After working my disdain for them out on the drawings with a stiletto, I fixed belfries in Siena, baptisteries in Ravenna, balconies in Verona, columns in Rome, towers in Pisa, secret passages in Salo. I made churches safe for gangsters and communicants and black-clad crones who scratch you with their yellow nails if you dare to take their front row seats on days when the saint’s dusty blood is liquefied in a vial. 
            In Trievo, the remains of the structures that awed the populace in their hovels still stand, testimony to the agile skill of the architect in the service of power. Arches present their light and shade, as do long arcades; columns and towers cast shadows, and monumental walkways seem to float in the air; the white stone is blackened here and there, monogrammed, numbered, graffitied with the marks of emperors down through senators, prefects, mayors, civil servants, fascists, communists, scooter boys, football hooligans. The market is said to have been built by a fondly remembered emperor, even though he was two at the time it was raised; people would rather remember him for markets he didn’t build than for the cack-handed battles he fought, or the time he blinded, raped and killed his sister in a fit of drunken melancholy. The steam-streaked walls of the baths stand too, where men were men and boys were nervous, where laughter rang out from some while others, by imperial decree, sat in tubs in the background and opened the veins in their wrists, eyes raised to see the goddesses. All the structures form a labyrinth leading to a triumphal way, that leads in turn to the marble pool once full of sacred carp, now the traffic roundabout.
            To make the steam for the baths, of course, what did my ancestors do? Swanky with their empire, and their manifestos from the gods, they cut down every tree for a hundred-mile radius. They laid the swamps bare, laid the town open to clouds of mosquitoes, odours, vapours.
            The town council wanted me to fix it all up at one time, had big blue plans drawn up, and parchment contracts, ridiculous wax seals at the ready. It would be Trievo’s fortune; people would flood in to see this jewel from the classical world. I saw the gaudy crowds outside for a second. They were so real I was about to throw the window up and tell them all to fuck off home. I suggested enclosing the whole town in chrome. The councillors looked at me. One of them commented, “It might be a little slippery in the rain,” and some of them tittered. They didn’t get it: they too would be under the chrome. I was giving them the futurist’s Pompeii, and what were they thinking of? The weather, like English grandmothers wondering if they ought to walk their dogs.
            I remember the boy. He is standing in my doorway under my carving of the Virgin of the Sancta Sofia disguised as Josephine Baker.
            “What?” I ask him.
            “Papa.” He hands me a note. I know when he speaks that it is from his mother. I open it and read the words, Allright, allright Iago, you are a very, very faithful man. I frown at the repetitions of words that say it all when used once. 
            “She’s been at her Shakespeare again,” I tell the boy. He copies my frown. “Does she want an answer?” He doesn’t know. “Do you know what it means?” I ask, and when he looks puzzled I show him the note. He shakes his head with the frankness of children. “I don’t know what it means, either.” I take money out of pockets, and give some to him, feel his eyes on me. “What are you looking at?” I tease him gently, and he shakes his head again, looks at the floor, but I know.
            My yellowed lizard eyes, and the scales covering black bruises that show under my skin, he sees; the clothes that hang off my frame, the sickness that picks at me but refuses to consume me.
            My metal bird lurches on its metal perch in its metal cage, and tries to sing. It needs oil, though; it sounds as if it is choking. Mercifully, it comes to a rusty halt. My look invites the boy to laugh, but the only change in his face is the emergence of his dry tongue, which rests at the side of his mouth.
            “Go,” I tell him. “And give my love to your mother and remind her that, as usual, I haven’t got a clue what she’s talking about.”
            “Thank you, Papa.” He bows his head, and is so eager to leave that he runs into a small girl in the doorway. She is dressed for tenement Sunday, and has wedding confetti in her hair. She brushes the boy aside with the early disdain of a lady, and stands, patient, waiting for me to invite her in. I remember her – I think it was her – the one who liked to sit in corners and doze and dream, and one time when I watched her eyelids flickering I saw behind them the red sheen of molten lead. All I see now though is her white wall eye, almost fixed on me, then I spy the note clutched in her hand from her mother, and reach into my pockets.
