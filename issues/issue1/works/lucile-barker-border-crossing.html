The bus coaches came up every two weeks, full of seniors from Maine, Vermont, even New Hampshire. Mr. Wei loved it and dreaded it. He was the pharmacist in Queen Mary, seven miles from the border. He was the man that they all loved because Canadian drug prices were so much lower than those in the Northern United States.

He had already made up some of the standard prescriptions: two months' worth of Losec for heartburn, three months' worth of Glucophage for diabetics, a month worth of Rogaine for high blood pressure. His assistant, Sheila, had boxes of inhalers ready to have labels printed and slapped on them. Singulair tablets for asthmatics as well.

Mrs. Wei tapped him on the shoulder.

"You have to do it," she muttered and he shook his head, not wanting the assistant to hear.

He pushed her further behind the counter.

"Just put him on the bus," she said and then realized her nephew was behind her, even though the area behind the counter was forbidden territory.

"Half those people are senile," he snarled. "The only one to worry about is the bus driver. Just put me beside one of the Chinese people and slip the driver a hundred. No one will check."

Mr. Wei would have liked nothing better than to be rid of Hangji, who preferred to call himself Hank. He was tired of the boy, tired of what this was doing to his wife. As for his sister-in-law, he wanted to tell her to come and get her child, take him back to Finjan. For twenty, Hangji was too young. The English was nearly perfect but there was something missing. At that age all the Wei children had known what they wanted, even Laura, who was doing Shakespeare in a park again this summer. All Hangji wanted was a place, New York City, but his visa was only good for Canada. For some reason he blamed Mrs. Wei for this, not understanding that New Brunswick was part of Canada, not the United States.

The bus pulled up in front of the drug store and the seniors piled out. Both sides had it down to a science. Everyone lined up in alphabetical order, their prescriptions at the ready. When they had handed them in, they went off to the St. Alban's Inn for lunch and then shopping, which was the stated purpose of the trip. Some even went curling or just played bridge in the lounge in the summer. At three-thirty the drugs would be in neat, white paper bags, with the bills stapled to them. All in order. Except for Hangji who was disturbing the alphabet and their lives.

The bus driver was new and wasn't very happy. For a group of seniors, these people were too noisy. He was used to driving a school bus where he could yell at the kids. He believed in the American way and all these people were going against it. He didn't like Canadians. You could never tell who they were. Just look at that druggist, probably just off the plane and already had his own store.

Wei's Drugs and Sundries, since 1969. Who were they trying to kid? And why did these people line up so patiently like sheep? Pavlov's dogs, he thought, getting themselves in alphabetical order.

"They've upped your dosage," Mr. Wei said to Mrs. Anderson and she nodded.

Bell, Bouvier, Carter, Cartwright, Emory, Findlay. This was one of Mr. Wei's favorite busloads, people who were well enough to stay well. He felt such satisfaction. There was a group from Inch Valley that seemed to be on their last legs. Every few months, there would be a few more missing, replaced by new customers who were just as old and frail as the ones who had disappeared.

And Hank had disappeared, even though he was supposed to be washing the floor out back in the storeroom. Mr. Wei glanced up and saw Hank through the front window, talking earnestly to the bus driver, but there were another seventy prescriptions to go, and some of them were for drugs that these seniors hadn't had before, so he needed to find the instruction sheets he had made up for them. If you develop a rash or shortness of breath… He could feel himself panting slightly between the exertion of getting this all done, and the stress of Hank. Later, he told himself, I'll deal with it later.

The elderly people cruised back in, some slightly tiddly from the wine they had had at lunch.

"Don't take your new medication for at least three hours after drinking," he warned Mrs. Talbot, who giggled.

He didn't see Hank get on the bus, but the boy was gone. So was about three hundred dollars U.S. from the cash drawer.

"That won't get him very far in New York," said Sheila, hugging Mrs. Wei, who was sobbing.

"What will I tell my sister?" she asked, her tears soaking into Sheila's blue smock.

There was no call by seven, so Hank must have gotten across. There was nothing to tell.
