Hundreds of moths flee before me
   Ephemera.  Lesser lives, cut like dandelions
   or strangled in air filters.

   The dog won't move.
   He's become immune to the threat and the grass is cool.
   Old bones disallow fear of the growl or the shocking impact
   of fallen branch on blade.

   September wind is blowing hard up here today in Michigan.
   Canceling summer like a bad check.
   Clouds and football roll over the city.
   A passing bird casually heading south, a faint whiff of ice.

   I can hear the big lake breathing, forty miles away.
   Coughing up snow we'll soon find falling.
   On breakers and swells, the last boats tow fish and fat drunks between the piers.
   The sun descends into water, thoughtfully considering the nature of cold.

   Gas her up again, prime, pull to start.
   The deck's too high, but it doesn't matter.
   Let a couple moths fly... blown out the gap between blade and grass.
   Unshredded and heading straight up into the diminishing light.

   I haven't seen the damned geese yet.
   But they're in flight, hundreds of miles north of here, in fog and rain.
   Feel the steady beat of their wings on this wind?
   I tend to hold them responsible

   For this oncoming rush of air
   For the grey sky that's no longer gold
   For this grass that must be cut
   For another season when it will grow
   For the lake that will no longer breathe
   For the moths which fall like flakes
   
   They must know this is no place to be.
   Not while the wind is up and things begin to fall
   so thoughtlessly from the sky.
