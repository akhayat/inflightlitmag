between two pueblos,
in a tiny worn-out town
named Peña Blanca, or
white rock, a place no one
quite knows about,
in 1997 which was
a year with little rain,
in a backyard where
soft thick dust crusted
every dry blade of grass,
and the wooden fence
of an old horse corral
sat decaying in the shade
of a great cottonwood
whose dry fallen trees
layered five inches deep
would crunch underfoot
with even light steps,
a rickety ladder and some
rotting wooden pallets
and many rusted tin cans
were the best playthings
a little girl could ask for.

the pallets were the deck
of a private pirate ship,
and one loosened board
formed the plank that
she would walk along
until the fateful moment
when the ocean of leaves
would receive her with
a familiar crisp sound,
and the rusted cans were
telescopes that focused
far away, or they were
jars of pickled fruits that
she was glad she didn’t
have to share with anybody
else, and the ladder led to
a crow’s nest formed from
a crook in the trunk of
the cottonwood tree
where she would sit and
look out on the sea of white
dust and wonder if anyone
would ever show up
and try to board her ship.
