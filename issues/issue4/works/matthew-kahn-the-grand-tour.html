The first thing you see is a white guy with dreadlocks
playing the didgeridoo for spare change in Prague,
while pigeons peck red-lit vomit—
fat, ratty-feathered birds, perched on the heads
of statuary saints.

And you think, “this is where Kafka lived”
as if his presence signified something beyond itself,
beyond the mere tautology of stating that something is, was,
has been.

As if to travel were to outrun yourself,
to slip off solipsism’s sticky skin.
As if the secret to life lay in a sixth century synagogue
or a vaulted cathedral or a dive bar with dogs and cheap beer.
As if the zeitgeist, the world-spirit, the last barricade to reality,
the barrier between the numina and the merely nominal,
could be breached by the miles passed.

So you set out on a quest,
for Truth, or Knowledge, or whatever is Real,
only to feel the same sun in another country.
You flee by air, by land, by bus or plane or ferry,
certain that what you want is hidden in your next stop.
But instead you find:

A white guy with dreadlocks,
playing the didgeridoo.
