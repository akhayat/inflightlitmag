La Colorada was a small village in the middle of the Mexican desert. A small piece of dry earth haunted by bad weather and trapped in the past. The outside world was a story, something that happened to a friend of a friend; a mysterious neighbor rarely allowed in.
Its citizens used to work as perfectly as machinery, everyone fitted so well into their job and into each other that the entire city was one huge engine, running day and night, every little piece as important as the next. Arms entwined so tight it seemed impossible to let go.
They had been too busy surviving to notice time threatening to jump up their fences.
One morning, on their way to the fields, the men found an old man with a big, foreign hat sitting on the grass right in front of the biggest mountain. “What are you doing on the ground old man?” they asked him “Are you traveling? Come to one of our houses, we’ll feed you and our kids will play with you.” But the old man politely reclined “I Can’t move, I’m waiting for Rezende, he´ll come down this mountain any day now,” he declared, then turned his head and didn’t mutter another word.
After a few days they got used to his presence and stopped trying to take him home, instead they regarded him simply as a spot of the landscape. They would wave at him, he would wave back, the sun would be up, the man sitting, the grass down.
And weeks passed, and the seasons changed, and they would keep passing him by every morning on their way to work “Still waiting old man?” “Maybe you have the wrong mountain” They would joke, call him crazy, make fun of him, but he never lost his temper, all he would say was, “I’m just waiting for Rezende, he’ll come down that mountain any day now to take me.”
They would discuss the old man at their parties, they didn’t understand him and it made them nervous, and they would laugh at him, and the kids would joke and knock at the bathroom door, “C´mon hurry up! What are you doing in there? Waiting for Rezende?”
But so it happened, that one idle Monday on their way to work the old man wasn’t there anymore, all they could find was the hat he used to wear, now cold from the morning mist.
“He did it!” They all cheered “Rezende came for him!”
After that day Rezende became an extra habitant on all homes. “If you don’t finish your vegetables you can’t go wait for Rezende when you grow up,” parents would say. And the men bought hats and quit their jobs and went to sit there too, day and all night waiting for Rezende.
Trouble entered the city around that time. The fields were abandoned; so the neglected animals began getting stolen, getting eaten by coyotes, getting too thin, dying. Things that had always flowed as naturally as water in the village were now becoming a problem: food, work, love.
And the wives were unhappy for the first time, they started yelling; this made men began to feel uncomfortable in their homes so they would leave earlier and return later. The mothers were so upset that they would take it out on their kids. The kids couldn’t understand why, but they started fighting each other, skipping school, they were hungry too and that put them in a new mood, they started stealing, then they started hating themselves for doing it. They felt on both ends of a war they never meant to fight.
The old grandmothers worried and went to the mountain to build a beautiful shrine so Rezende would like them better and side with the women. So females were spending their entire day out there also, making the monument pretty, getting fresh flowers, giving away the love no one else was taking. They left their homes too.
And the village, like the cattle, got too thin and began to die. Sad to see their old lives a skeleton, the people rarely came back.
Now if you pass by the mountain you will find a line of men in white hats sitting on one side, waiting, and a breathtaking monument surrounded by women out of breath on the other. The waiters.
Meanwhile, out of their view, the ghost city is waking up, the kids, sons of nobody, never understood so they made peace, they plant, they water, they care. Trees start growing, houses get painted, food grows again and songs get singed.
The kids hung a sign at the entrance of the city “Rezende”. And now, their heats warm, they don’t wait, they live.
