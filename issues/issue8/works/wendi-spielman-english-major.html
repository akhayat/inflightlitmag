After our mother’s suicide, my sister wrote a poem about the symbolic nature of her death. She had a prescription pill addiction, and to kill herself, she took one hundred and twelve pills. My sister’s poem was something in the vein of “he who lives by the sword dies by the sword,” et cetera, et cetera.
It’s been a year since our mother’s death now, and I have finally learned that there was not a single symbolic thing about it. I’m not sure if my sister still thinks there was. But our mother killed herself with those pills because they were there, where they always were, on her bedside table. They were the simplest, most effective method to get her where she wanted to be. There was no poetry in the vomit we found her in. No symbolism in her cold blue skin. Her death, if it was anything, was all realism, some of the finest ever constructed.
