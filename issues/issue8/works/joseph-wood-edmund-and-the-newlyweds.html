It was in the Spring of 1972 that the newly married Mr and Mrs Bridges moved into their California apartment. It was a modest, modern place in an unassuming three story block. Within a week they had met and been made welcome by each of the tenants.

They were riding the bus to the supermarket when one of these tenants - a man whose height and stature might have made him seem imposing were it not for his warmth and intelligence - got on the bus also.



They waved to him from where they were sitting and he sat on the seat behind them, placed the bowling ball bag he was carrying down next to him. The Bridges turned and chatted with him a while until he said that this was his stop and he looked forward to seeing them around soon.

He stood and picked up his bowling ball bag they all said a quick goodbye and Mrs. Bridges told him, "Get a strike for us, Ed."

This is a story about perspective. Of your own and of others'. And it ought to be read on the bus.

Not at home. There you can read anything you want without impeachment. Without the paranoia that someone might be wondering what you're reading. Without the fantasy that someone might be reading over your shoulder.

Not on the train either. On the train no-one cares that you're reading. The Tube likewise; on the tube no-one cares. The train and the Tube, they're impersonal.

And the beer garden, even that won't do. Despite the gin-nosed drunk asking, despite the staggering divorcee insisting, no-one cares what you're reading there either.

On the bus, of course, no-one cares either. But on the bus there is a chance that perhaps they might. On the bus, at least, you can think that they might. You can even think that they do. There is space for it in the gaps where hot breath is shared. These gaps on trains are too wide, these gaps on the tube too ignored; lost amongst the constant changing and rearranging of the carriage's contents. These gaps in the beer garden are clouded with smoke and desperation.

They are all impersonal and so there is no potential for anything personal to be violated. A secret shared on the train, on the Tube, in the beer garden, it remains secret.

But the bus, the bus is intimate claustrophobia. They say that school prepares you for work. No. School prepares you for the bus. Trapped amongst peers of disparate interests. Shoulder to shoulder. Breathing in each other's breath. Stepping on each other's feet. Waiting, avoiding eye contact and conversation, until you can leave.
