She sunk into the cool edge of the pool, the water and April air draping her in the cool velvet of the night. The water lapped around her shoulders. The pseudo-jungle in the suburban prairie shuddered in the dim glow of the outdoor lightbulb. The birds chattered above on the powerlines, speed-mating in an attempt to fulfill their biological purpose. Small, tinny sounds of musical lamentations about home and sex, adventure and love, accompanied the birds out of a speaker. She could see them waltz against the night sky, silhouetted against the dark periwinkle curtain of wind; unmarred by the glistening stars lightyears away.

She clung to the edge of the pool, staring her legs scissoring back and forth under the liquid haze of the chlorinated pool. Her thighs and toes bobbed through the bright blue water. Her back faced the house, she instead stared at the created outcropping of rock and the not-quite-native palm trees. She watched as she watched the bird, in the half seconds before she blinked, the manifestations of her anxiety seemed to dance with the birds. Anxiety was a prisoner from an old film who rattled a tin cup against cell bars, begging, screaming, and wailing for attention. He screamed about her body, the only thing she felt like she could have complete control over, telling her she was sick, everything on her was falling apart into rapid old age. That she was going to become a burden on those around her; that she would run out of time before her time even began. The only way to momentarily pacify him was to go to the doctor, an expensive coping mechanism that left her chewing her thumbnail in waiting rooms contemplating how to save every cent. And even then it did not always help, her trust of doctors had been poisoned by the media in a way that had no antidote.

But the anxiety did not only manifest in the form of copays and prescriptions. He also came in all the forms of the quarter-life-crisis; every authority figure who ever expected something of her, the boy who she had given everything to and still left her with the sour taste of swallowed sobs in her mouth, and friends who seemed indifferent at best. She even saw her potential future, a former low-grade whizkid turned pinnacle of middle class mediocrity.

As she watched her body under the water, she could not help but feel disconnected from it, like it was someone else’s entirely that she was borrowing, a bodysnatcher in her own right with shitty luck. Even at that, she felt guilty about her self-indulgent sadness. Everyone her age seemed to have similar feelings, masked behind the sunny Instagram posts—a universal sense that their world was burning around her and she was holding the lighter. Her head sunk into the water before the tears could ripple the pool’s surface. Everything became muffled in the sanctity of the chlorine. She wondered how long she could stay there. She wondered who would drown first, her or her sadness.
