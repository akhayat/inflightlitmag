mid-June grit sits in my teeth
as I gnaw the smoke filled wind
tasting fire on my lips, watching
    shreds of blonde chamisa bloom,
torn and tossed
    like the matchstick trees stripped and
lost into black glass earth, when
    not one lick of mercy from the flames
graces bone-dry branches. My glances
fill with grains of sand. I beg
for rain that never comes.

but the desert-thick dust on my
tongue forms a prayer that
rushes out of my lungs with the hot,
sick, thick air of disaster,
    asks for winds to whip faster and
stronger through these longer days
    of deep red, smoke-soaked sun
into shorter nights, when the
    summer-scratched corners of my
eyes catch the deep-fried mountain
ridges flaring up into rivers of light,
blowing me away.
