It began with a gift for a one hour massage, a gilded paper certificate given by Janey’s mother as a 40th birthday present. In the past, these certificates had found their way to the junk drawer and moldered past their expiration date. But her 39th year had held a twenty-pound weight gain and the navigation of work and life stress—like she was some battered canoe, unmanned on a white rapid river—unlike anything she had ever imagined. Life was unrelenting, and she knew it sounded like one of those terrible motivational posters, but she couldn’t find the second part, always delivered in elegant italics about how to get back up and persevere. She just couldn’t.  And she didn’t want to talk to anyone about it. It was just such a fucking chore to live through it, honestly. It took every single ounce of energy just to wake up and go about the business of living a life that sucked in nearly every way. She didn’t want to think about being broke at the end of the month and still needing to pay for flute lessons for Josephine, or registering her dog again at the court house and providing verification the dog didn’t have rabies. She didn’t want to think about enduring a work dinner with her husband present, and hearing his canned laughter. He was more a roommate than a husband. Let’s be honest. Let’s be really fucking honest. They were not the same people who had married one another twenty-one years ago, and she was done pretending that anything was going to change. So they went to Luke’s baseball games, and Josie’s choir concerts, and sat down to dinner and tried to throw themselves into work and parenting, and they went to bed at different times to avoid each other, and Janey almost hoped that he’d have an affair so she had a definitive, no nonsense ending to it all.

She’d given up running when her knees and hips revolted, but now she walked, or really alternated between walking and standing dumbly, lost in not thinking on the Constitution Trail while super fit men and women biked and ran and rollerbladed past her. She heard them coming up and zipping or huffing past and she wondered about their lives and marriages, if they sucked as bad as her own. But her hip, it ached, how it ached, like a bad tooth needing a root canal, and stretching just helped briefly and she was taking more Tylenol than she should, and it wasn’t really helping.
On Thursday morning, she woke, and she made coffee and made lunches, and she didn’t kiss her husband good-bye and he didn’t notice, and once the bus came and the house was empty of her children, she sat down and had a beer and cold, left over burrito for breakfast because it was what she wanted, oddly, and then she uncapped another beer and called in sick for work. It had been three years since she called in sick. She nursed her beer, peeled the label off slowly and with great precision, imagining some professional restorer trying to get a priceless stamp off of an envelope of no consequence. She waited until 9:00, when the spa was open, and she called to make an appointment.
For today, she told the receptionist. As soon as possible.

“Can you be here at 9:45 for paperwork with a 10:00 massage appointment?”

In the parking lot she cried. The spa was in a sad strip mall, and an obese woman in shorts, a white tank top, and flip flops, waiting by a bus stop sign watched her calmly, head on, until Janey put her head on the steering wheel to hide her face. Tanner had revealed her body to her, and it was a marvelous, mysterious gift. But it was terrifying too. Because where could she go after being read in such a way? Touched in such a way? She white knuckled the steering wheel, her keys still buried at the bottom of her purse. She caught her own eye in the rearview mirror. Yesterday’s mascara seemed a smudged message. Surely not home, where no kindness awaited her, not even from her own damn self.
