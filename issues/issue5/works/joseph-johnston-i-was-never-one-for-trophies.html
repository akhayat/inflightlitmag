Eric's nipple fell off today.
    He sure rattled the locker room three weeks ago. What a gesture for a seventeen-year old backstroker whose father won't even let him drive. Pierces his nipple with a safety pin on a Tuesday afternoon before swim practice, the February cold and pool chemicals cauterizing and cleansing the wound so his mother can't find out and rein in her precious small town punk, so sexy to my invisible freshman eyes.
    I watched him from the far corner, flicking that antiseptic Zippo and floating the unsafe portion of the safety pin through the campfire warmth of the flame back and forth, each pass increasing excitement among the swimmers while secretly purchasing courage. Showmanship. The bearded majority team captain finally called his bluff so we looked away and then heard a pop and a choir of gasps. He did it. A one-inch safety pin through the smooth pink punctuation of his bronze left pectoral, ringed with the coarse black hair of burgeoning manhood. I wanted to kiss it and make sure he was okay but I just stared into the rusting orange locker of my rookie season and caught my breath and pretended I wasn't impressed with shaggy-haired sham rebels.
    Swim practices began to roll into one another through winter darkness, me pretending not to steal glances at that righteous pin tunneling through Mt. Olympus, each day reinforcing how much of a coward I am. Then today the mercury rises and a rare strawberry spring allows us out on the quad for an impromptu early March lunch recess. Cooped up boys become mountain rams, wrestling for a spot on the picnic tables spelling out the initials of the school. Look, I hate sports. I hate the patriarchal pyramid of sports. My high school querencia is the art room and swimming is just a solitary way to appease the jocks in my life. I never fight. I always fly. But seeing proud Eric as reigning king of picnic table mountain made me want IN. I tossed my sketchbook therapist to the ground and charged, over a fat kid welder from shop class and atop the shoulders of the dry docked JV quarterback. I grabbed that safety pin through his white tee and pulled with a sharp twist widdershins.
    He'll never forget me now. I have his nipple. Woke up with it on the sodden grass of the fast-thawed quad, still pierced by the pin, framed by a blood-stained scrap of Marlon Brando t-shirt. I was never one for trophies before today.
