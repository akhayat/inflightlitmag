The Sexton lies toes up, stiff and cold
like every morning, staring at the room’s ceiling
in the Chelsea Hotel, not like other mornings.
A rare trip out of town. The wedding
of a dead friend’s daughter.
Not much else could coax him
out of his routine, but to be a proxy parent
he tolerates the throngs, the chitter-chat.
The Sexton has always loved people
a few at a time. In mobs? Not so much.
And now he’s slept where Janice Joplin
offered Leonard Cohen kindness
in the prehistoric era. The Sexton shaves
for once, he dons a rental tux, cleans up
when there’s a reason. He remembers
how good Dad-work works. He misses
the friend he’s standing in for. He hopes
his substitute back home will fill the hole evenly.
Please wait, he would warn the man,
‘til the two or three truly bereaved
leave the grounds before finishing.
He thinks of what to say, a toast for the reception.
His friend’s girl, a Xerox of the friend
and the bright guy she’s crazy over.
“Stay crazy,” he will tell them, “Don’t fry the relationship
with sun-power through a magnifying glass.
If it functions, don’t you mess with it.”
And then the clink of fancy glasses. Hear, hear.
The Sexton in New York, in a posh place
he would never spring for voluntarily. A kid calls
for her father. Someone has to answer
for the ones who have already left the world.
Someone puts their shovel down,
drives the intervening states,
walks the aisle, blesses the future.
