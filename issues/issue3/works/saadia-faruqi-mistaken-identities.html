They were an odd couple who probably turned heads and raised eyebrows everywhere they went. He was short, middle aged, spectacled and too brown to be labeled Hispanic, with a full beard that screamed danger. She was tall, willowy and younger than him by a full decade, dressed in tight-fitting jeans and an open-necked collared shirt that looked somehow offensive next to him. As if she was trying to annoy him, although he didn’t look annoyed. Just silent.

They walked into the convenience store together, not asking the attendant to fill the tank of their aging Volvo outside, just browsing the shelves casually. They searched the neatly packed bags of Lays potato chips and the packaged cheese snacks laid out like a regiment on its way to battle. They looked over the cans of sodas and Gatorade saluting an invisible general, but stayed away from the beer case. It didn’t seem as if they wanted to buy anything, but the store certainly wasn’t one that encouraged window-shopping. That’s when the attendant got suspicious.

“Hey you! Yeah I’m talking to you! Are you Moos-lim?” He was a spindly young man, a boy almost, with buck teeth and a grating voice. He pronounced his last word as if chewing tobacco and spitting it out, barely able to hide his derision. Behind his beady eyes a gleam of anticipation was apparent, heady at finally finding a target for his boiling emotions. His mother’s escape to New York, his father’s beatings, everything would be erased now that there was a real life Moos-lim in front of him. They were a rare breed in this part of Kansas, where even the blacks and Mexicans were frightened to stay too long.

The bearded, spectacled man stared in the attendant’s direction for a long minute. Thirty seconds turned into sixty. Buck Teeth grew nervous. He coughed and shifted his vision to the woman, standing with one hand on her hip near the paper goods aisle. “What’re you doing with this guy, lady?” Spit flew out in tiny droplets and rested on his lips, shimmering, waiting for something, anything. “You don’t want to be mixed up with no Moos-lim, you know. They’re terrorists and they beat up their wives.”

The woman glared. “I am his wife, and he’s very good to me. So you keep your filthy mouth shut, you hear?” Her voice was strong, loud, with a slight tremor that belied her brave words. Who was she scared of, him or the silent would-be terrorist?

The bearded man took her elbow and pushed her forward gently, away from Buck Teeth and his calculating gaze. They walked together towards the restrooms silently, footsteps in perfect sync despite their height difference. Buck Teeth left his cubicle and quickly followed them, hanging on to his anger by a tiny thread. “Where’re you going? You’ll need a key to the bathrooms and I ain’t giving no dirty ragheads the keys. You’ll probably do some kind of devil worship over there or something, or piss all over the floor like those Indian people.”

The duo continued without turning around, although he imagined the woman trembling at his temerity. They were pretty cool, Buck Teeth thought, impressed despite himself. He’d have started a fist-fight at lesser words. Perhaps they were used to this manner of speech, they who stood out like sore thumbs in this two-bit white Kansas town. Or perhaps they guessed that his jeering were just bravado fueled by a lack of breeding and an abusive father.

Buck Teeth stared after them, opening his mouth once more to heckle but the front door opened again, distracting him quite effectively. A man stood at the threshold, dressed in a lumpy blue sweatshirt with hands in pockets in a classic stance that reminded him of countless small budget movies he’d seen in the back room. No, not those movies, the other kind full of police chases in which gas stations actually blow up. His neck hair raised, his bluster disappeared, he froze where he stood, feeling an urgent need to pee like the boy he was.

The intruder sauntered in as if he owned the place and everyone inside. “What’s your name, kiddo?” he asked softly, like a teacher speaking to his favorite student in the playground. Wise, polite, nothing like a robber with a concealed gun. Buck Teeth swallowed nervously, eyes bulging out of their sockets like a fish on its last few breaths. “Sssam.”

“Well, Sssam, can you get the cash register open, please? That’s a good boy.” He raised his voice only slightly, but it seemed to boom. “And you two behind the shelves can come out now!”
Sam turned around slightly, carefully, shaggy brows drawing together as he struggled to put the pieces of a particularly vexing puzzle together. The Moos-lim and his lady were part of the robbery? He hadn’t seen that coming. Well, he had maybe pictured the man with a gun at first, but his silence had unnerved him. He thought terrorists were brash and rude, all ready to die for their sun-god Allah.

He couldn’t get his feet to move, worried that he may faint or worse, pee his pants. That wouldn’t look good on the camera, and Lisa, the girl at the laundromat next door certainly wouldn’t like to know he was a scaredy cat. There were terrorists in his store, goddamit, and he was going to defeat them. They may even call him on the evening news and give him a medal.

The bearded man and his not-so-lovely companion came out from the shadows, faces harsh and mouths open with indignation. The young woman spoke quickly, in a low tone full of venom. “Larry, what do you think you’re doing? We’re not going to rob this store, you idiot, I just wanted to go to the bathroom!”

Larry smiled a ghost of a smile. “Oh I’m going to rob it alright, my dear sister, you just wait and see.” There was a movement behind them, and they all turned dramatically and bit unsteadily. Buck Teeth Sam had decided to dash to his little cubicle in a valiant attempt to – do what? He didn’t get farther than two feet; the gun inside the blue sweatshirt was very real, and it went off with a sound that made his bladder finally let loose.

Sam lay stunned in a sickly sweet pool of yellow liquid on the floor of the convenience store, so badly shaken that he couldn’t really tell if he was dead or alive. The lady in the tight jeans was screaming her head off, which he found pretty funny in a stressed-out sort of way. The bearded man was as silent as ever, eyes blinking slowly like a lizard’s. He realized after a mental reconnaissance of his body parts that he was physically okay. Either Larry was joking around or he was a really bad shot, because the bullet had missed him, thank Jesus, and lodged into the wall behind his head. He hoped somebody outside had heard the commotion; he didn’t want to sit in his pee for longer than necessary.

Larry held a hand up to silence his sister. “Shut up, you bitch. I’m not going to spare this idiot’s life twice.” Then to Sam, “You, last chance, open the register and get me all the cash.”

Sam struggled up valiantly, but the woman pushed him down. “Keep sitting boy. Don’t you dare get up from that floor!” She turned to the would-be robber and wagged her finger at his face in a way that thrilled Sam’s heart. Now this was a lady he could call his own. “Don’t you see, Larry? People are going to look at Kamaal and think the worst of him. Why would you put him in this situation? Why would you do something that’s going to get his face in all the newspapers and on television?”

Larry’s smile was as cold as a shark’s. “Exactly.” Buck Teeth Sam shivered, unsure whether it was from fear or pneumonia.

Kamaal the silent bearded one leapt up in a sudden move that shocked Sam to his core. He instinctively wrapped both arms around himself in the universal symbol of protection, like a little armadillo protecting itself against the big, bad world of maniac drivers. After a millisecond he realized that the other man’s move wasn’t as fast or as courageous as he had hoped. There was no dynamite, no assault rifles. Kamaal was just leaning forward, nothing else. How odd. This family drama was going to be the death of him.

Buck Teeth Sam trembled slightly, lying back in the wetness that was starting to stink, not wanting to set the terrorist off. He fixed his eyes on Kamaal’s mouth, waiting to finally hear his voice. He was suddenly yearning to know what Kamaal sounded like.

When the voice suddenly came, it was beautiful and mellifluous, even though Sam couldn’t have identified that second word or its meaning in all his lifetime. “Larry, please. I don’t wish for any trouble. I don’t wish to be hauled off to jail because of my looks or what the police assume.” The words were mild, the tone even milder, the accent very New York. What the hell? Didn’t all bearded eastern men come from Saudi? Sam released his breath with a whoosh, realized that he’d been hoping for a hero. This guy wasn’t it, thought. This guy had his own problems.

“What’re you going to do about it, you camel jockey?”

The lady hissed, Kamaal held up his hand to stop her. “Darling, no. It’s okay.”

Darling? What was this, the 1950’s? Buck Teeth Sam couldn’t take it anymore. It’s not like anyone else was sitting in the stench of their own urine. He looked around for a weapon, didn’t find any. He’d have to do it the hard way then. This afternoon soap needed to end before someone got killed.

He hauled himself up fluidly, noting his own ballerina-like grace with astonishment and awe. He picked up a chair lying hidden behind a shelf, not caring that Larry was aiming the gun right at him. He swung the chair with full might, not hitting a damn thing, but finally finding out what a bullet felt like. Burning, eating away at heart muscle, oozing pain and surprise in equal measure. In his last faltering breaths he felt disappointment, sad that Larry would get a day’s worth of cash from the register, sad that the lovely lady would see her fears realized, that Kamaal would be accused of murder. Sad that he would never get to see Lisa naked.

Then, as his eyes glazed, he remembered the camera in the store’s ceiling, bearing witness to the real criminal and the real hero.
