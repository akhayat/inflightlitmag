There are no pretensions to fashion where I live.
Farmers work hard –
Rear children, crops and (sometimes) smiles.
Farmhouses tend toward practicality. Utility.
No CinemaScope tweeness of a stage Irish dream.

Ranges are the hearts that beat in every house
Practical little beasts –
The pit ponies of fire –
No thoroughbred Agas here.
A stalwart Rayburn or a Belling
Will heat your water for you,
Dry your clothes and cook your food.

The range here at the farm gave up after ninety years of use.
Friends – both strong and brave of heart – stripped it down and laid it bare
Three quarters of a ton of iron and steel
I mourned its loss as though a friend had died.

It was the centre of our family life:
My mother roasted stews – thick, dark and full of love –
And swooped with floured hands to hot griddles – cooking  soda breads
In thick, fragrant slices –
Soaked, by us, with pale salted butter –
Sweet and hot to the taste.
A kettle rested on the top – fussing and whistling in a permanent boil.

But my strongest memory - and one of nurturing love –
Was of coming home from school when I was young.
On winter days, we'd tumble in the door – socks dirty, mouths agape like quarrelling hungry birds –
My mother would be waiting – eyes bright, skin flushed with heat –
To place us at the table one by one.

The range door opened with a searing blast
And out came hand-made pancakes crisp and sweet.
Placed in the range, before we'd cycled home,
The stacks of honey discs shimmered like a mirage –
Glossy with butter, sprinkled with sugar – light as our mother's kiss,

Each bite was a scalding heaven;
We'd bite, howl, chew – then howl again.
But plates were emptied and forks sucked clean.

I still remember those pancake days; three little girls chattering like doves.
And making sure we had all that we needed –
My mother – flushed with happiness, heat and love.
