Stale winds gushed through the trees, licking the frozen branches before kissing the Hunter’s flesh. He stood poised with his bow ready while the snow cloaked his menacing visage. Although his body was still, his mind was hysteric, screaming with sorrow and rage bridled in his chilled skull.
It had been exactly one month since his son stood beside him in the woods learning to aim his small bow. He was not with the Hunter, not looking at him in innocent admiration, nor would he ever be. The boy was now buried under the snow and dirt, plucked by the judgeless hands of Death.
The Hunter lowered his bow to chip the frozen tear off his face. The cold became him, numbing his body into a fearsome statue once more while he waited for his prey. He would worry about his son standing in the cold, but the boy stood adamant amidst the frozen woods. The boy would hold his bow still for a long time before he shuddered as the freezing breeze forced his breath out. That was always when the Hunter knew to take him home before he caught a cold.
Food was never as important than his son’s health.
But it was not the cold that caught him, it was cancer. His Father stood by his bed, as motionless as he would while hunting, like a sentinel over his son’s dying body.
The boy’s absence made the woods expand and the cold wind screamed louder. The air gored his lungs as he inhaled and his eyes were involuntary sealed by the falling snow and sudden lashes of frost. Gently yet forcefully, he turned his head, looking for movement beyond that of the cyclonic snow ensnaring him. Although the small faun did not move, the Hunter saw him, brown and full of life amid the dead, frozen expanse.
The faun was already staring at the Hunter, fearless; yet caution swam in its warm eyes. The Hunter knew not to move, but would have to wait until the faun was unaware, even for a moment. It was always more painful for the both of them when the prey watches the Hunter take it.
His son could never kill an animal, either he never wanted to or he simply could not. There is a curious feeling that sinks in, knowing you are taking life away. Even through countless hours or decades of cautious creeping and silent watching while stalking the prey, death only happens in an instant. Despite the long and quiet battle between the Hunter and the game, everything happens once the last breath is drawn. It is the moment that time itself vanishes from you that you die. That is true death.
The Hunter would never force His son to fire his bow, even if he had a clear shot, He knew that firing the bow required true intent. He knew the boy was scared of Death, which made watching him die an agonizing battle in which he could do nothing. Looking at the monitor and the dim green lights bounce up and down with an unyielding beep.
His son’s eyes had been shut for days while he was on life support and the cancer crept through him starting at his throat. The Hunter could never forget His son’s brown eyes, they carried a longing to live and experience life but also a tranquil glare that made him seem at peace with Death.
Sometimes the boy would shudder in his bed as he did in the subzero woods. He would often be the first to find the animal and tell his father where it went, either to help his father or just to hasten the hunt, both were achieved.
Even now as the Hunter gazed at the Faun he could hear His son’s meek voice whispering of how beautiful it is, but perhaps it was a rogue draft playing a cruel trick. After steering away from the Hunter, the faun fleetingly pranced through the trees. The Hunter crept behind in the darkness of the trees, like a phantom hidden in the shadow of the faun, clinging to it with every step in the storm.
The faun stopped and looked at the Hunter once again, and began eating red berries off a bush, the only berry that seemed to survive winter, and the only lively red that the snowy wasteland had seen… for a moment at least. The Hunter often set a trap by those berries; they had a distinct smell that always drew in the prey. It was an odd scent for a human as it reeked of chemicals for miles, like the peculiar scent of chlorine intertwined with sanitizing solution.
As the faun ate it watched the Hunter, seemingly less afraid of Him. The smell of the berries ushered itself into the Hunter’s nose, making Him remember the scent of the hospital, the new scent of His son. The Hunter stood staring at His son while the doctor sanitized his hands before adjusting the son’s feeding tube. The Father watched expressionless as His son’s life faded by the second.
The doctor told the Father that feeding him would be futile, marking the boy as dead. The boy’s body would no longer digest food, leaving him to fade into the cold arms of Death.
Hunting was soothing for the Father, not sitting in the peaceful serenity of the woods, no, but watching life disappear from his prey had the ability to make Him forget His pain, and remember death is always the denouement. Life is but a penultimate strand of pain, motley with love until you are claimed by the hand of Death. That is all Death requires. Man is born to die; he must hold his will to live every day, making life seem an arduous task juxtaposed to death.
The Hunter poised his bow, stringing a coal black arrow into its position. The faun gazed at him and bowed its head for a moment before raising it once more, its brown eyes rested on the Hunter.
Time was still for a moment in their exchange. The faun watched the Hunter while the arrow was let loose, soaring cruelly through the bitter winds.
It continued to look at the Hunter’s empty eyes, two green orbs that flickered between the flurries of falling snow. The arrow sent vibrations through the fawn’s body as it entered diagonally through its nose and out the back of its neck, locking its jaw shut but not killing it.
The Hunter let His bow down. His eyelids dropped and He felt weak as He watched the faun jolt to life and leap away with the arrow lodged in its head. The Hunter was angry that it was not a fatal shot, the Faun’s suffering weighed on Him greatly.
His son would have been furious with Him for hurting the faun. His last words to the Hunter were to only hunt when He needed food and never to resolve His boredom through killing. The Hunter had to hunt today; it was His effort to forget carrying his son’s coffin. For such a light coffin, it was the heaviest thing the Hunter would ever carry.
The faun left tender footprints in the snow, scarcely breaking past the first layer. The Hunter hounded the trail in hopes to end the faun’s suffering as it was sure to die without being able to open its mouth to eat. His body deadened while the wind marshaled the muffled cries of the faun into His ears.
Not very much farther the Hunter saw the faun sitting with its front legs curled beneath him, sedentary and at peace it seemed. Its mouth opened as far is it could, jittering the arrow and causing the faun to close its eyes in pain, reopening them slowly to see the Hunter before him.
Snow circled around Him as he stood tall and dark. The dead trees behind him did not creek and the wind silenced itself.
The Faun bowed its head to the Hunter.
The Hunter knelt down to the Faun.
At that moment, it was as if all the sorrow in the world was given a voice. “I’m sorry,” the Hunter wept, his bony body shaking. “I’m so sorry.”
