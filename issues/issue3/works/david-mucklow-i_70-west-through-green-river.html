This place’s principle business
is passing through,
and the gas station
at the interstate exit
seals the fate of the
local carpenter and roofer now forgotten,
the last screw in the drywall
now the nail in their broken life’s coffin.

The sign outside the only restaurant
will always read closed,
the faces of locals
refusing to leave
are livid and lost.

Barely between here
and that city,
rural life is barren
even for a highway town,
and your thoughts of buying local are
rude attempts to help strangers.

The truckers are indifferent,
and the gas station clerk’s smile is fleeting
as she pours you old black coffee.

She says have a good day,
(maybe a good life, you think)
and those blue eyes are pretty,
but you, too
is all you reply.
